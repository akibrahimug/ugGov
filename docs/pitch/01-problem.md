# The problem — why Uganda needs GOV.UG

## What a citizen sees today

Search "Ministry of Health Uganda" or "how to apply for PDM". You will find:

- Several lookalike WordPress sites, some active, some abandoned.
- Different designs, different logos, different navigation patterns.
- Inconsistent use of the Uganda coat of arms and flag colours.
- No cross-government search. Google is the de-facto search engine for public information.
- Little or no accessibility: missing alt text, low contrast, no keyboard navigation, broken on cheap Android phones over 3G.
- No canonical "how-to-apply" guidance for most services — a mix of FAQ pages, PDFs and social media posts.

## Five concrete pains

### 1. Fragmentation &amp; brand chaos
Uganda has **~75 major MDAs** (ministries, departments, agencies) and hundreds of local government bodies. Roughly **80% run independent WordPress sites**, each with its own theme, host and vendor. The result is a public digital estate that looks nothing like one government.

### 2. Fake-site and phishing risk
When every public body has its own domain with its own look, citizens cannot distinguish genuine from fraudulent. A single, memorable, trusted home makes phishing dramatically harder.

### 3. Accessibility gap
Most current sites fail **WCAG 2.2 AA** on the most basic checks. Rural users on entry-level Android phones over 3G — the majority of Uganda's internet population — bounce before first paint. **Accessibility is a national-inclusion issue, not a nice-to-have.**

### 4. Duplicated spend
Every MDA pays for its own hosting, theme, developer and designer. Conservatively, **tens of millions of shillings per year are spent across Government on redundant web infrastructure**. Consolidation is a cost story before it is a quality story.

### 5. Poor service discoverability
There is no canonical answer to "how do I enrol in the Parish Development Model?" or "how do I get a National Identification Number?" Citizens cobble together information from unofficial blogs and WhatsApp. This undermines the uptake of flagship programmes.

## The one number that matters

For the cost of re-theming five ministry websites separately, Uganda can stand up **one** unified platform that serves all of them — with better design, better accessibility, better search, and one shared cost base.

That is the opportunity.
